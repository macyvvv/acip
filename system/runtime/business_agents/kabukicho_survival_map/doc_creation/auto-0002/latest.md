**Arriving in Kabukicho with luggage? Here's what catches tourists off guard — and how to plan around it**

### Post Body

Landing in Shinjuku and heading into Kabukicho with luggage? Two things that trip people up most:

**1. Coin lockers are often full by 10 AM**

The nearest large locker bank is JR Shinjuku Station B1F (East/Central East Gate). The station has ~3,600 lockers total, but large lockers that fit a standard 24" suitcase typically fill up early in the day during peak tourist season. Here's what to know:
- Medium lockers (¥500–600): fit a 20" carry-on  
- Large lockers (¥700–900): fit standard 24" checked luggage  
- **The math:** arrive before 10 AM if you're checking in late, or plan a backup (nearby capsule hotels like Booth Net Cafe & Capsule let you drop luggage for a small fee even if you're not staying overnight).

**2. International ATMs—which ones actually work**

Seven Bank ATMs (found at most 7-Elevens, including three within 2 minutes of Shinjuku Station) are your most reliable bet. They accept:
- Visa, Mastercard, Amex, UnionPay, JCB, Maestro, Cirrus, Discover, Diners  
- ~¥110 fee per withdrawal  
- ¥100,000 withdrawal limit per transaction  
- Multilingual menus (Japanese, English, Chinese, Korean)

Other ATM networks in the area can be pickier about foreign cards, so if you have a choice, go Seven Bank.

**The actual survival part:**

Our map does two things other tourist guides don't:

1. **It flags essentials by time-sensitivity.** Coin lockers aren't always available (especially morning/afternoon). Toilets and konbini are. You plan differently if you know which is conditional.

2. **It updates when venues actually change.** The Tokyu Kabukicho Tower's gender-neutral toilets were reversed to separated facilities after public backlash — most old listings still show the original design. We catch those reversals so you're not surprised when you arrive.

**Practical first-hours checklist:**
- ✓ Locate a locker (or plan lodging/overflow) before noon

## Channel 3: Budget/Backpacker Communities (Facebook Groups/Discord)

### Post Title
**Net cafes vs. capsule hotels vs. standard lodging in Kabukicho — what's actually legal and what protections you get**

### Post Body

Sleeping in a Kabukicho net cafe or capsule hotel? Here's what you need to know before booking.

**The legal gray zone (and why it matters):**

Net cafes and capsule hotels operate in Japan legally, but they're *not* licensed under the same lodging regulations as hotels. That's not a reason to avoid them — it's a reason to know what you're booking:

- **Capsule hotels** (e.g., Booth Net Cafe & Capsule, Shinjuku Kuyakusho-mae Capsule Hotel): overnight stays from ¥3,000–3,700. Reviewed, established venues in Kabukicho. No hotel-business-act license, but consistent operations and public reviews.
- **Net cafes with overnight options** (e.g., Booth): net-cafe seating from ¥600/2hr, capsule space ¥3,000/night. Showers are a separate paid add-on (¥300/30min with towel). Open 24/7, common with traveling workers and backpackers.
- **Standard hotels**: higher cost, licensed lodging protections, same standard amenities. Shinjuku has many close to Kabukicho if you want traditional hotel safety guarantees.

**Budget breakdown for a night in Kabukicho:**

| Option | Cost | What you get | Catch |
|--------|------|--------------|-------|
| Capsule hotel | ¥3,000–3,700 | Sleeping pod, sometimes shared facilities | Non-refundable if no-show |
| Net cafe overnight | ¥600–1,000/2hr, or flat rate | Reclining seat, free drinks/Wi-Fi, shower (¥300 extra) | Might be loud; shared space |
| Booth combo (net cafe + capsule) | ¥3,000 capsule OR ¥600/2hr cafe | Flexible: work a few hours, then sleep | Best for flexibility |

**Reality check:**

Dozens of backpackers use these options nightly without issue. But because they're unlicensed as hotels, there's no mandatory cancellation insurance or business-act protections. If you're risk-averse, pay extra for a licensed hotel. If you're budget-focused and OK with a less formal setup, net cafes and capsule hotels are established, legal options — just know going in that you're not getting a hotel booking receipt.

**Our recommendation:**

Use our map to find reviewed, established venues only — no sketchy recommendations. Cross-reference reviews on Tripadvisor/Google Maps before booking. Budget lodging in Tokyo is competitive; you can find good options at all three tiers.
